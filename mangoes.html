What is a mango?

Mangoes are tropical stone fruits that grow on large evergreen trees. They have smooth skin, golden to deep orange flesh, and a single flat seed in the center. Their flavor ranges from intensely sweet and floral to slightly tangy, depending on the variety and ripeness.

Mangoes are believed to have originated in South Asia and are now grown in many warm regions worldwide.

Today, mangoes are enjoyed fresh, blended into drinks, cooked into savory dishes, and turned into desserts, pickles, and chutneys. They’re a symbol of summer in many cultures and often associated with celebration and abundance.

Popular mango varieties

There are hundreds of mango varieties, each with its own flavor, aroma, and texture. Here are a few well-known types:

Alphonso

Often called the “king of mangoes,” Alphonso is rich, sweet, and intensely aromatic with smooth, fiberless flesh.

Kensington Pride

A popular Australian variety with a sweet, slightly tangy flavor and a juicy, fragrant flesh—great for eating fresh.

Tommy Atkins

Widely available, with firm flesh and mild sweetness. Holds its shape well, making it useful in salads and salsas.

Honey / Ataulfo

Small, golden mangoes with creamy, buttery flesh and a honey-like sweetness, perfect for smoothies and snacking.

Nutritional benefits

Mangoes are not just delicious—they also provide a range of vitamins, minerals, and beneficial plant compounds.

Key nutrients

  • Vitamin C: Supports immune function and helps with collagen production.
  • Vitamin A: Important for vision, skin health, and normal growth.
  • Dietary fiber: Aids digestion and helps you feel full and satisfied.
  • Folate & B vitamins: Support energy metabolism and cell function.
  • Antioxidants: Such as beta-carotene and other carotenoids.

Enjoying mango in moderation can be part of a balanced diet—especially when paired with other fruits and whole foods.

Culinary uses

Mangoes shine in both sweet and savory dishes. Their natural sweetness and acidity make them incredibly versatile in the kitchen.

Sweet ideas

  • Blend into smoothies or lassi-style yogurt drinks.
  • Layer into fruit salads, parfaits, or chia puddings.
  • Use as a topping for pancakes, waffles, or ice cream.
  • Make sorbets, popsicles, or simple mango purée for desserts.

Savory ideas

  • Toss into salsas with lime, chili, and herbs.
  • Add to salads with leafy greens, avocado, and nuts.
  • Cook into chutneys to serve with curries or grilled foods.
  • Use in rice dishes, grain bowls, or fresh spring rolls.

How to choose, ripen, and store mangoes

Choosing ripe mangoes

  • Feel: A ripe mango gives slightly when gently squeezed, like a ripe peach.
  • Smell: A sweet, fragrant aroma near the stem end is a good sign.
  • Look: Color can help, but ripeness is more about feel and fragrance than skin color alone.

Ripening at home

  • Room temperature: Leave firm mangoes on the counter until they soften.
  • Paper bag trick: Place mangoes in a paper bag to speed up ripening.

Storing mangoes

  • Whole, ripe: Store in the fridge and use within a few days.
  • Cut mango: Refrigerate in an airtight container and enjoy within 2–3 days.
  • Freezing: Dice and freeze on a tray, then store in bags for smoothies and desserts.
